# Geocentrism

The astronomical model in which the Earth sits at the center of the
universe and the celestial bodies — including the Sun — orbit around it.
The dominant model from antiquity through the 17th century, finally
displaced by [[Heliocentrism]] after [[Galileo Galilei|Galileo]]'s
[[Telescope]] observations and [[Johannes Kepler|Kepler]]'s mathematical
laws made the alternative empirically and quantitatively superior.

## Variants

- **Ptolemaic** — the most refined version, formalized by Claudius Ptolemy
  (~150 CE) in the *Almagest*. Used epicycles (small circles whose centers
  trace larger circles) to account for retrograde planetary motion while
  preserving the central, stationary Earth.
- **Tychonic** — [[Tycho Brahe]]'s hybrid (1588): Earth at center, Sun and
  Moon orbit Earth, but other planets orbit the Sun. Mathematically
  equivalent to [[Heliocentrism]] but preserving theological priority of
  a stationary Earth.

## Why it persisted so long

Geocentrism was not merely religious dogma. It had genuine empirical
strengths that the 16th-century alternatives lacked:

- **No observed stellar parallax** — if Earth moved, nearby stars should
  shift seasonally. They did not (parallax wasn't measured until 1838,
  long after the heliocentric debate was settled on other grounds).
- **Aristotelian physics** — falling objects appeared to confirm a
  central, stationary Earth as their natural resting place.
- **Naked-eye observations** — without the [[Telescope]], the planets
  looked like points of light moving against the stars; nothing visible
  to a 16th-century eye demanded a heliocentric explanation.

Telescopic observation broke this. [[Galileo Galilei]]'s discovery of
Jupiter's moons proved that not everything orbits Earth, and the phases
of Venus required Venus to orbit the Sun — incompatible with the
Ptolemaic system but consistent with both [[Heliocentrism|Copernican]]
and Tychonic models.
