# Heliocentrism

The astronomical model in which the Sun sits at the center of the solar
system and the planets — including Earth — orbit around it. Replaced
[[Geocentrism]] as the consensus model over the course of the 16th and
17th centuries, though resistance from religious and academic institutions
continued well into the 18th.

## Historical sketch

- **3rd century BCE** — Aristarchus of Samos proposes a heliocentric model;
  the idea is rejected in favor of [[Geocentrism]] for ~1800 years
- **1543** — Nicolaus Copernicus publishes *De revolutionibus orbium coelestium*
  on his deathbed, reviving heliocentrism with circular orbits
- **1609** — [[Galileo Galilei]] uses the [[Telescope]] to observe Jovian
  moons, phases of Venus, and lunar mountains — empirical evidence
  inconsistent with [[Geocentrism]]
- **1609-1619** — [[Johannes Kepler]] derives his three laws of planetary
  motion from [[Tycho Brahe]]'s observations, mathematizing heliocentrism
  with elliptical (not circular) orbits
- **1687** — Newton's *Principia* explains Kepler's laws via universal
  gravitation, sealing heliocentrism as the physically correct model

## Why it took so long

Heliocentrism was harder to defend than it might seem in retrospect. Three
challenges had to be overcome:

1. **Stellar parallax** — if Earth orbits, the apparent positions of nearby
   stars should shift seasonally. Parallax is real but tiny (the closest
   star, Proxima Centauri, has a parallax of 0.77 arcseconds), and it
   wasn't measured until 1838.
2. **Falling-object problem** — pre-Newtonian physics couldn't explain why
   objects fall straight down on a moving Earth. Resolved by inertia.
3. **Religious authority** — the Catholic Church and most Protestant
   churches held [[Geocentrism]] as theologically required. The
   [[Galileo Galilei|Galileo trial]] (1633) made this conflict acute.

## Connections

- [[Tycho Brahe]] proposed a hybrid model preserving central Earth while
  having other planets orbit the Sun — a compromise that died once
  [[Johannes Kepler|Kepler]] mathematized true heliocentrism
- The instrument that decisively shifted opinion was the [[Telescope]]
